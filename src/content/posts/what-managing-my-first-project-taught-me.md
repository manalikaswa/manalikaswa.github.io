# What Managing My First Project Taught Me

A year ago, I got the opportunity to lead my first cross-functional project.

It wasn't just another release. The project involved coordinating multiple teams, navigating unfamiliar technical and operational challenges, working with stakeholders across different locations, and figuring out several processes from scratch. Around twenty people were involved, and we had roughly a month to go from planning to launch.

We made it.

Looking back after a year, these are the lessons that stayed with me.

> Disclaimer: These are simply my observations on what worked well and what I'd do differently next time.

## What Worked

### Own the Outcome

The biggest mindset shift was understanding what ownership really means.

I adopted a simple mindset:

> You and only you are responsible for the outcome. No blame game. If something goes wrong, you are supposed to freaking fix it.

### Communicate Relentlessly

Communication became one of the biggest force multipliers.

Within the team, we had regular sync-ups, but just as valuable were the informal check-ins throughout the day. A simple "Where are we stuck?" often surfaced problems before they became delays. Asking questions early helped reduce uncertainty and improve planning.

Outside the team, frequent updates kept stakeholders informed, made approvals faster, and allowed us to raise risks before they became surprises. Even small updates helped maintain alignment.

One lesson I learned was that overcommunication is usually better than undercommunication.

### A Shared Sense of Urgency

Every minute mattered.

One particular day still stands out.

We discovered that an important approval needed for the launch had to be submitted before the end of the day. The catch? We barely understood the process.

At the beginning of the morning, we were still figuring out what documents were required, who owned which part, and how the submission even worked.

For the next few hours, everyone switched into problem-solving mode.

- Some people researched documentation.
- Others reached out to colleagues with prior experience.
- Some prepared the required deliverables while others verified everything before submission.

Just before the deadline, everything finally came together. Looking back, what amazes me isn't that we met the deadline.

It's how naturally everyone stepped up. Nobody waited to be told what to do. Nobody said, "That's not my responsibility."

People simply looked for the next blocker they could remove.

That's when I realized that urgency isn't created by pressure. It's created by shared commitment.

### Respect Commitments

Estimating timelines for unfamiliar tasks is hard. Sticking to them? Even harder.

Frequent follow-ups helped. Sometimes the conversation was simply understanding what was blocking progress. Sometimes it meant finding additional help. Occasionally, it meant escalating issues when timelines were genuinely at risk.

Balancing empathy with accountability was important. People were working hard to make sure we released on time, and it mattered — everyone took their commitments seriously.

### Shamelessly Asking for Help

One of the most valuable lessons was learning to ask for help early.

There were several situations where we simply didn't have enough knowledge within the team. Instead of spending days trying to figure everything out ourselves, we reached out to people with experience. Many of them had never worked with us before, yet they generously shared their knowledge and helped us move forward. A fifteen-minute conversation often saved hours of trial and error.

Lesson: Don't hesitate. Ask.

### Iterations, Iterations, Iterations

We broke things down, front-loaded work, and parallelized. This helped catch failures early and gave the team momentum.

We had small iterations with a bunch of features being rolled out every 2–3 days. Multiple rounds of testing gave us confidence while reducing the risk of unpleasant surprises near the deadline.

### Right Attitude in the Team

There was pressure. There were long days. But there was also laughter.

And there was *junoon* — that infectious energy that makes people go the extra mile because they genuinely care.

Whenever something broke, nobody's first reaction was to complain. It was, "Let's figure this out."

That attitude spread across the team.

---

Looking back, if I had to summarize my approach, it would be:

- Influence over control.
- Trust over supervision.
- Alignment over hierarchy.

I was contributing alongside the team, not just coordinating it. Finishing a task and asking, "I'm done with this. What's left?" built far more trust than assigning work ever could. I made sure to be dependable, available, and willing to help in whatever way I could — without drawing boundaries around my role. This was especially important because I wasn't managing anyone directly. How else were people going to take me seriously?

## What I'd Do Differently

Of course, not everything went smoothly. Here's what I'd do differently next time.

### Embrace Ownership Earlier

Initially, I kept looking for validation.

Every decision felt significant, and I wanted someone more experienced to confirm that I was on the right track.

Eventually, I realized that ownership doesn't always come with certainty. It means making the best possible decision with the information you have.

### Spend More Time Thinking

Most mornings started with discussing what needed to be done that day.

What I didn't spend enough time doing was stepping back and asking bigger questions.

- Where is the real bottleneck?
- What assumptions are we making?
- What part of the process do we still not understand?

A little more planning each day could have prevented several avoidable delays.

### Communicate the Why

The team understood what needed to be done and when.

I'm not sure everyone understood why it mattered.

A stronger shared purpose could have created even greater ownership, encouraged better ideas, and invited more proactive feedback.

### Document While It's Fresh

We solved many unfamiliar problems.

Unfortunately, we documented very few of them. Like many teams, we kept saying we'd write everything down later. Later never came.

Future us deserved better.

### Continuous Ownership

This is something I still struggle with — and honestly, I'm not good at it. Ownership isn't a one-time responsibility; it's a continuous commitment. It continues after launch — tracking outcomes, prioritizing improvements, following up on issues, and making sure the solution continues to succeed. You need to prioritize, take action yourself when needed, and step in — even if that just means sending a reminder email or setting up a meeting.

The deadline ends. Ownership doesn't.

---

When I think about that today, I don't remember the deadlines.

I remember the people.

- People stepping outside their roles.
- People helping teammates they'd never worked with before.
- People choosing optimism when frustration would've been easier.
- People who cared.

That's probably the biggest lesson I took away. Great projects aren't built by perfect plans. They're built by people who care enough to own the outcome together.

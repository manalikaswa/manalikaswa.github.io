# Why I Decided Not to Pursue an MBA from a Top B-School

## The Crossroads

A few days ago, I was thrilled to be selected for XLRI Jamshedpur's flagship Business Management (BM) program after months of late-night study sessions, scoring 99.54 percentile in the entrance exam, and acing the interview. A dream come true.

I, however, took a tough call to not pursue it.

This blog outlines key deciding factors of mine — useful for anyone who is in a similar position, or just curious. Each factor is tagged as Short-term or Long-term, depending on the lens it requires. These are my personal reflections, not universal truths — treat them as just one data point among many.

## Why I initially wanted to Do an MBA

### Learning

- (Short term)
- Learn and explore the Business side of things (The kind of understanding that helps you build your own company)
- Peer Learning: What happens when 200 smart people from diverse backgrounds spend 2 years learning together?

### Career pivot

- (Long term)
- Get into Product management and eventually into Tech leadership
- Gain front office and strategy opportunities
- Executors/Decision makers/Innovators are less replaceable, and an MBA often opens such roles

### Network

- (Long term)

### Career longevity and flexibility

- (Long term)
- A general MBA can open doors to new industries and roles that stay relevant throughout your career.
- It offers longevity — CXO roles, and boardroom influence.

### The "Tag"

- (Long term)
- Cracking a top B-school shows intelligence, drive, and potential. Like it or not, the tag gets you noticed.

## Why I decided Not to Do It?

### Placements

- (Short term)
- Since I wanted to get into Product, I focused exclusively on tech companies. However, they've been hiring fewer people — even from top B-schools — and the numbers have been declining (around 10% of the batch, based on placement reports).
- B-school placements can feel random, and you might end up in a company or role that doesn't align with your long-term goals.

### What I was Giving Up

- (Short term)
- Opportunity cost: The high tuition fees plus lost income.
- Leaving a job I enjoy, with perceived opportunities for growth.

### AI and global job market volatility

- (Short and long term)
- AI is reimagining roles, organizations, industries and the broader market. A great article on this shift: The Rise of Ultra-Generalists. While front-office and execution roles may be less affected by this, the overall job demand and supply could become volatile.
- Consulting, once a stronghold, is changing, firms like Bain and McKinsey have slowed down hiring (A good article on consulting and AI)
- Fewer people might be needed to create the same impact (unclear whether this means layoffs or greater impact)

### Uncertainty if the "Tag" will matter

- (Long term)
- People say an MBA is a long-term investment (say 10 years) — but who knows what will be relevant in 2035? Will the degree still carry the same weight in the future with changing times?

## What I'll be doing instead?

- Being good at what I do: Staying in Tech, but building my expertise around software architecture (Not just writing code :P)
- Self-Learning: Studying business, leadership, and product via books, content and communities (P.S. I'll also be sharing what I learn through writing!)

## Final Thoughts

Absolutely. An MBA is far from irrelevant — in fact, it continues to open doors. If you're:

- Seeking better compensation
- Aiming for a role upgrade
- Planning a career pivot into a new industry
- Looking for a lot of learning

And finding it hard to break in through other means, then few options are as structured and high-leverage as a Tier-1 MBA.

Just do your research well. Talk to recent alumni and current students. Carefully weigh what you're giving up against what you will gain.

I'd suggest thinking about the short term of a 5–7 year horizon — because in the long term, the corporate landscape could look very different.

Thanks for reading!

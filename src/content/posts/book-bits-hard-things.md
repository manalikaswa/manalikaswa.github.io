## Personal Learnings

- There are no shortcuts to knowledge, especially knowledge gained from personal experience.
- I (i.e. Ben) ended up moving in multiple social circles and hanging out with kids with very different outlooks on the world. It amazed me how a diverse perspective utterly changed the meaning of every significant event in the world.
- **Friends:** You need two kinds of friends in your life. The first is one you can call when something good happens, and you need someone who will be excited for you. The second is somebody you can call when things go horribly wrong — when your life is on the line and you only have one phone call.
- Spend zero time on what you could have done, and devote all of your time to what you might do.
- **Courage:** What is the difference between being yellow and being brave? No difference — only what you do. They both feel the same. They both fear dying and getting hurt. The man who is yellow refuses to face up to what he's got to face. The hero is more disciplined; he fights those feelings off and does what he has to do.
- **Partnership:** With Marc and me, even after eighteen years, he upsets me almost every day by finding something wrong in my thinking, and I do the same for him. It works.
- There is always a move.

## Leadership Learnings

- Ability to get someone to follow you even if only out of curiosity.
- Has a high sense of purpose and cares deeply about the work she does.
- The ability to build trust over time is often the difference between companies that execute well and companies that are chaotic.
- Great CEOs face the pain. They don't quit.
- CEOs possess a different set of data, knowledge, and perspective than anybody else in the company. The only reason the CEO can make a better decision is her superior knowledge — of technology, prior decisions, culture, personnel, and more.
- **The ability to articulate the vision:** Can the leader articulate a vision that's interesting, dynamic, and compelling? More important, can the leader do this when things fall apart?
- **The right kind of ambition:** Must get really great people to work for her. Smart people do not want to work for people who do not have their interests in mind and in heart. The CEO cares more about the employees than she cares about herself. A huge number of employees believe it's their company and behave accordingly.
- **The ability to achieve the vision:** competence, pure and simple. A CEO must be accomplished enough or smart enough that people will want to work for her — and should never be so confident that she stops improving her skills.

### Leadership Responsibilities

- **Take care of the people, the products, and the profits — in that order.**
- **Story and strategy:** Gives meaning to the specific work that people do, aligns interests, enables decision-making, and provides motivation. Answers the question *Why?* Builds context.
- **Decision-making:** with intelligence, logic, and courage. There is never enough time to gather all the information needed to make a decision. You must continuously and systematically gather knowledge in the company's day-to-day activities.
- **Hold people accountable:** for their efforts, promises (commitments), and results.
- **Don't put it all on your shoulders:** I thought that it was my job and my job only to worry about the company's problems… the whole organization needed to understand why, so that we could together fix the things that were broken.
- **Designing and implementing the communication architecture** for her company: organizational design, meetings, processes, email, Yammer, and even one-on-one meetings with managers and employees. If you want people to communicate, the best way is to make them report to the same manager. With any design, you will optimize communication among some parts of the organization at the expense of others. Prioritize. Optimize the organization for the people doing the work — not for the managers.
- **Culture:** a way of working. Ensure that critical operating values persist, such as delighting customers or making beautiful products. Shock is a great mechanism for behavioural change. (Amazon had desks made of doors to embody 'value for customers'.)
- You should have an opinion on absolutely everything — every forecast, every product plan, every presentation, and even every comment. Let people know what you think.
- Separate the importance of the issues from how you feel about them. A thousand things can go wrong and sink the ship. If you focus too much on them, you will drive yourself nuts and likely crash your company. Focus on where you are going rather than on what you hope to avoid.
- Ensure free-flowing information.
- Build strict processes for potentially political issues and do not deviate: (1) performance evaluation and compensation, (2) organizational design and territory, and (3) promotions.

### Peacetime vs. Wartime CEOs

The world looks one way in peacetime but very different when you must fight for your life every day. In times of peace, one has time to care about things like appropriateness, long-term cultural consequences, and people's feelings. In times of war, killing the enemy and getting the troops safely home is all that counts.

- **Peacetime CEOs:** focus on expanding. Broad creativity. Focus on the big picture and empower people to make detailed decisions.
- **Wartime CEOs:** strict adherence and alignment to the mission. Cares about a speck of dust on a gnat's ass if it interferes with the prime directive. Let the war define the culture.

### Building a World-Class Management Team

- At some point the monolithic design of one huge organization runs out of gas and you will need to split things into smaller subgroups.
- **Setting expectations:** Do you expect them to hold regular one-on-ones? Give performance feedback? Train their people? Agree on objectives with their team?
- A company will be most successful if senior managers optimize for the company's success (global optimization) rather than their own (local optimization). As an employee, why would I want to work long hours to advance the career of my manager? Nothing motivates a great employee more than a mission so important that it supersedes everyone's personal ambition.
- **Good managers** are measured by: (1) results against objectives, (2) building a strong and loyal team, (3) innovation, (4) being effective at communicating, supporting, and getting what they need from others on staff.
- Content-free executives have no value in startups. Every executive must understand the product, the technology, the customers, and the market.
- In engineering meetings under pressure to ship on time, everybody hopes for good news. When the facts don't align, a clever manager will find a narrative to make everybody feel better — until the next meeting. They are lying to themselves: taking action only on positive indicators and seeking alternative explanations only for negative leading indicators.
- **Performance management — white box, not black box:** metrics are incentives. By measuring quality, features, and schedule and discussing them at every staff meeting, my people focused intensely on those metrics to the exclusion of other goals. Penalize managers who sacrifice the future for the short term and reward those who invest in the future even if that investment cannot be easily measured. Anything you measure automatically creates a set of employee behaviors.

### Be Mindful of Kinds of Executives

- **Ones — setting the direction:** like spending most of their time gathering information from a broad variety of sources, from employees to customers to competitors. Love making decisions. Sometimes get bored with important execution details — process design, goal setting, structured accountability, training, and performance management.
- **Twos — making the company perform at the highest level:** insist upon super-clear goals and strongly prefer not to change goals or direction unless absolutely necessary. Where a One might happily spend a day reading, studying, and thinking, that would make a Two very nervous — it wouldn't feel like work. Big decisions worry Twos much more than Ones.
- **Small-company executive:** nothing happens unless you make it happen. You have to take eight to ten new initiatives a day or the company stands still. You must run a high-quality hiring process, have terrific domain expertise, create processes from scratch, and be extremely creative about initiating new directions and tasks.
- **Big-company executive:** spends most of the time optimizing and tuning the existing business. Most skilled big-company executives will tell you that more than three new initiatives in a quarter is trying to do too much. Tend to be interrupt-driven, and become very good at complex decision-making, prioritization, organizational design, process improvement, and organizational communication.

## Management Learnings

- Two ways for a manager to improve the output of an employee: motivation and training.
- **Motivation:** Are your employees excited to come to work? Do they believe in the mission? Do they enjoy coming to work every day? Are any actively disengaged? Do they clearly understand what's expected of them? Do they stay a long time or quit faster than normal? Why do employees quit?
- If you manage ambitious people, from time to time they will want to expand their scope of responsibility.
- **Performance management:** Give people feedback because you want them to succeed, not because you want them to fail. People rarely improve a weakness they are unaware of. Be direct.
- **One-on-ones:** the free-form meeting for all the pressing issues, brilliant ideas, and chronic frustrations that don't fit neatly into status reports and email. Since it's the employee's meeting, the manager should do 10% of the talking and 90% of the listening. Ask: If we could improve in any way, how would we do it? What's the number-one problem with our organization, and why? What's not fun about working here? Who is really kicking ass? Whom do you admire? If you were me, what changes would you make? What don't you like about the product? What's the biggest opportunity we're missing? What are we not doing that we should be doing? Are you happy working here?
- In good organizations, people can focus on their work and have confidence that if they get their work done, good things will happen for both the company and them personally.
- In a poor organization, people spend much of their time fighting organizational boundaries, infighting, and broken processes. They aren't even clear on what their jobs are, so there is no way to know if they're getting the job done. In the miracle case that they work ridiculous hours and get it done, they have no idea what it means for the company or their careers.
- **Hard times are fun:** "Of all the times, the Darwin project was the most fun and the most hard. I worked seven days a week, 8 a.m.–10 p.m., for six months straight. And it wasn't just me — it was everybody in the office… It was a tight-knit group of people. A lot of the really junior people really stepped up. It was a great growing experience for them to be thrown into the middle of the ocean and told, 'Okay, swim.'" — Ted, an engineer
- **Exceptions to rules:** Since Dennis Rodman is allowed to miss practice, can other star players miss practice too? "Of course not. There is only room for one Dennis Rodman on this team. In fact, you can really only have a very few Dennis Rodmans in society as a whole; otherwise, we would degenerate into anarchy."
- **Promotion:** The process should yield a result similar to the very best karate dojos. To achieve the next level (e.g. brown belt to black belt), you must defeat an opponent in combat at that level. Start with an extremely crisp definition not only of the responsibilities at each level but also of the skill required to perform the duties.
- **Titles:** (1) Employees want them — while you may plan to work at your company forever, at least some employees need to plan for life after it. (2) Eventually, people need to know who is who.
- **Peter principle:** members are promoted so long as they work competently. Sooner or later they are promoted to a position at which they are no longer competent (their "level of incompetence"), and there they remain, unable to earn further promotions. Be mindful of when and whom to promote.
- **Law of crappy people:** For any title level in a large organization, the talent on that level will eventually converge to the crappiest person with the title.

## Product Learnings

- Figuring out the right product is the innovator's job, not the customer's job. Innovation requires a combination of knowledge, skill, and courage.
- Markets weren't "efficient" at finding the truth; they were just very efficient at converging on a conclusion — often the wrong conclusion.
- **Lead bullets (war over fights):** "Ben, those silver bullets that you and Mike are looking for are fine and good, but our Web server is five times slower. There is no silver bullet that's going to fix that." This was not a time to pivot. "There are no silver bullets for this, only lead bullets." We had to build a better product.
- **Competence:** Good product managers know the market, the product, the product line, and the competition extremely well, and operate from a strong basis of knowledge and confidence. A good product manager is the CEO of the product and knows the context going in (company, revenue, funding, competition). Bad product managers constantly want to be told what to do. Good product managers decompose problems; bad ones combine all problems into one.
- **Responsibility:** Good product managers take responsibility for devising and executing a winning plan (no excuses) and measure themselves by the success of the product. Bad ones have lots of excuses, and once they fail, they point out that they predicted they would fail.
- **Communication:** Good product managers don't get all their time sucked up by the various organizations that must work together to deliver the right product at the right time. They crisply define the target — the "what" (as opposed to the "how") — and manage its delivery. They create collateral, FAQs, presentations, and white papers that salespeople, marketing, and executives can leverage.

## Other Hard Things

### Hiring

- There is no such thing as a great CEO, a great head of marketing, or a great head of sales. There is only a great head of sales *for your company for the next twelve to twenty-four months.*
- Hire for strengths, not for lack of weaknesses.
- Hire people with the right kind of ambition. A bunch of high-IQ people with the wrong kind of ambition won't work. Intelligence is not the only important quality — being effective also means working hard, being reliable, and being an excellent team member.
- One excellent way to develop a high standard is to interview people you see doing a great job in their field. Find out what their standard is and add it to your own.
- Give new hires a list of people they need to know and learn from — then require a report on what they learned from each.

### Demotion

- If you walk into a demotion discussion with an open decision, you will walk out with a mess — of a situation and of a relationship.
- You must get comfortable with the thought that the employee may quit the company. If you cannot afford to lose him, you cannot make this change.
- Admit that if you were a more experienced CEO, you might be able to develop him into the role, but two people who don't know what they are doing is a recipe for failure.
- Acknowledge the contributions.
- If appropriate, move him to another area of the company where his skills, talent, and knowledge will help.

### Attrition

Three categories: (1) people who quit, (2) people who got fired, and (3) people who quit, but it's okay because the company didn't want them anyway.

### Firing

- Leaving a failing leader in place will cause an entire department to slowly rot.
- The wrong way to view an executive firing is as an executive failure; the correct way is as an interview/integration process system failure.
- Review any performance reviews or written performance conversations to understand any inconsistencies in your prior communication.
- The executive will be keenly interested in how the news is communicated to the company and to the outside world. It is best to let her decide.

### Layoffs

- During a time like this, it is difficult to focus on the future because the past overwhelms you — but that's exactly what you must do.
- Be present. Be visible. Be engaging. People want to see you, and whether you care.
- You are laying people off because the company failed to hit its plan. If individual performance were the only issue, you'd be taking a different measure.
- The people who stay will care deeply about how you treat their laid-off colleagues.
